\section*{}
\noindent Consider the matrix
\[
A = \begin{bmatrix}
3 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 1 \\
0 & 1 & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
\subsection*{(a) Characteristic Polynomial}
Determine the characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I_3)$ of $A$.

\begin{equation} \label{CharPoly}
    A-\lambda I_3=
    \begin{bmatrix}
3 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 1 \\
0 & 1 & 1
\end{bmatrix}-
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \lambda  & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & \lambda & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & \lambda
    \end{bmatrix} =
  \begin{bmatrix}
        3 - \lambda & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 - \lambda & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 - \lambda
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation}

\begin{equation*}
    \det(A-\lambda I_3) = (3-\lambda) \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 1-\lambda \end{bmatrix} - 0 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1- \lambda \end{bmatrix} + 0 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1- \lambda \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (3-\lambda) \cdot ((1-\lambda)\cdot (1-\lambda) - (1\cdot 1))
\end{equation*}
Definition 21.12~\cite{NUMLA}
\begin{equation*}
    (3-\lambda) \cdot ((1-\lambda)\cdot (1-\lambda) - (1\cdot 1)) = (3-\lambda) \cdot (\lambda^2 - 2\lambda)
\end{equation*}
Conclusion:
\begin{equation*}
    \det(A - \lambda I_3) = (3-\lambda) \cdot (\lambda^2 - 2\lambda)
\end{equation*}
\subsection*{(b-c) Some Eigenvalues}
Show that $0$ and $3$ are eigenvalues of $A$ and other values are welcome too.\\\\
The eigenvalues $\lambda$ can be found by finding the solutions to the characteristic polynomial from (a), since any $\lambda$ that results in the polynomials being 0 is an eigenvalue for $A$.\\
This means solving:
\begin{equation*}
    (3-\lambda) \cdot (\lambda^2 - 2\lambda) = 0
\end{equation*}
(With a hint from MatLab)  this can formulated to finding a solution to:
\begin{equation*}
    3-\lambda = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \lambda^2 - 2\lambda = 0
\end{equation*}
trivially a solution is $\lambda = 3$, making 3 an eigenvalue for $A$:
\begin{equation*}
    3 - \lambda = 0 = 3 - 3
\end{equation*}
For the other one $\lambda^2 - 2\lambda$, the quadratic formula can be used\\
 to find the roots for $1 \cdot \lambda^2 - 2 \cdot \lambda + 0 = 0 $.
 \begin{equation*}
    \lambda = \frac{-(-2) \pm \sqrt{(-2)^2 - 4 \cdot 1 \cdot 0}}{2 \cdot 1} = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4}}{2} = \frac{2 \pm 2}{2}
 \end{equation*}
This leaves two solutions
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{2 + 2}{2} = 2 \quad  \text{and} \quad \frac{2 - 2}{2} = 0
\end{equation*}
which means the the eigenvalues for $A$ is $ [3,2,0]$
\subsection*{(d) Basis using Eigenvectors}
Determine a basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.\\\\
Proposition 21.5~\cite{NUMLA}, says that if the eigenvalues of $A$ are unique and there is a set of eigenvectores for $A$, then the eigenvectores constitues a basis for $A$.\\
Eigenvectores can be found by solving for a $v$ in $(A - \lambda I_3)v = 0$ for each of the eigenvalues $\lambda \in [3,2,0]$\\\\
From equation\eqref{CharPoly} $(A - \lambda I_3)$ is already expressed.\\
For $\lambda = 3$
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        3 - 3 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 - 3 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 - 3
    \end{bmatrix} 
    =
     \begin{bmatrix}
        0 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 &  - 2 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 &  - 2
    \end{bmatrix} 
    = (A - 3 I_3)
\end{equation*}
add some $v \in \mathbb{R}^3 \neq 0$ (Does this notation even make sense?)
\begin{equation*}
    (A - 3 I_3)v =
 \begin{bmatrix}
        0 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 &  - 2 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 &  - 2
    \end{bmatrix} 
     \begin{bmatrix}
        x \\
        y \\
        z
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation*}
Note, there are many solutions for $v$ since $x$ can litereally be any value, but a basis needs to conist of linear independant vectors, which means only a single vector per eigenvalue is needed, hence the simplest vector that can be found is the best.\\
\begin{equation*}
    (A - 3 I_3)v =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 &  - 2 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 &  - 2
    \end{bmatrix} 
     \begin{bmatrix}
        1 \\
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation*}
The vector $\begin{bmatrix}
     1 \\
        0 \\
        0
\end{bmatrix}$ 
is an eigenvector for $A$.\\\\
For $\lambda = 2$
\begin{equation*}
    (A-2I_3) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        3 - 2 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 - 2 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 - 2
    \end{bmatrix} 
    =
     \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 &  - 1 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 &  - 1
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    (A-2I_3)v =
     \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 &  - 1 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 &  - 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        1 \\
        1
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation*}
For $\lambda = 0$
\begin{equation*}
    (A-1I_3) =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        3 - 0 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 - 0 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 - 0
    \end{bmatrix} 
    =
      \begin{bmatrix}
        3 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    (A-1I_3)v =
 \begin{bmatrix}
        3 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 & 1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        -1 \\
        1
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        0 \\
        0 \\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} 
\end{equation*}
Now for the eigenvalues $[3,2,0]$ we have the following eigenvectores $\begin{bmatrix}
    1 \\ 0 \\ 0
\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}
    0 \\ 1\\ 1
\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}
    0 \\ -1\\ 1
\end{bmatrix}$
Which constitutes the basis for $A$
\subsection*{(e) Panic?!}
Write $A = V \Lambda V^{-1}$ for suitable $V$ and diagonal $\Lambda$.\\
Following Proposition 21.6~\cite{NUMLA} with the resulting basis for $A$ from (d) and the eigenvalues from (b-c).\\\\
\begin{equation*}
    V = \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 & -1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
    \qquad 
    \Lambda = \begin{bmatrix}
        3 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2 & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & 0
    \end{bmatrix}
    \qquad V^{-1} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\
        0 & -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
The inverse of $V$ was calculated using python and includ a sanity check for $A = V \Lambda V^{-1}$
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
# Defining the Diagonalization of A
V = np.array([[1,0,0],
              [0,1,-1],
              [0,1,1]])
Vinv = np.linalg.inv(V)
Lambda = np.array([[3,0,0],
                   [0,2,0],
                   [0,0,0]])
# Sanity check for A
A = V @ Lambda @ Vinv
print(A)
#Output -->
[[3. 0. 0.]
 [0. 1. 1.]
 [0. 1. 1.]]
\end{lstlisting}
hence $A$ is Diagonalizable.

\subsection*{(f) Witness me! - Said Donald Duck Calmly}
Use item (e) to determine $A^k$ for all $k$.\\\\
Item (e) is the factorization of $A = V \Lambda V^{-1}$, refering to Theorem 6.3.2~\cite{LinearAlgApp} remark 4:\\
\textit{If $A$ is diagonalizable, then A can be factored into a product $V\Lambda V^{-1}$}\\
it then follows from remark 4 that
\begin{equation*}
    A^2 = (V \Lambda V^{-1})(V \Lambda V^{-1}) = V\Lambda^2 V^{-1}
\end{equation*}
This leaves $A^k = V\Lambda^k V^{-1}$ which can be refined further by focusing on the right side.
\begin{equation*}
    V\Lambda^k V^{-1} =  \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 & -1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & 0^k
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\
        0 & -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
Let's split this Mammoth of matrix multiplicaiton
\begin{equation*}
    V \Lambda^k = \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 1 & -1 \\
        0 & 1 & 1
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0 \\
        0 & 0 & 0^k
    \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
Then multiply by $V^{-1}$, remember $\frac{2^k}{2} = 2^{k-1}$
\begin{equation*}
  V\Lambda^k V^{-1}  = \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0 \\
        0 & 2^k & 0
    \end{bmatrix} 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\
        0 & -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2}
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^{k-1} & 2^{k-1} \\
        0 & 2^{k-1} & 2^{k-1}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
In conclusion $A^k$ can be expressed/determined using item (e) as such
\begin{equation*}
    A^k =  \begin{bmatrix}
        3^k & 0 & 0 \\
        0 & 2^{k-1} & 2^{k-1} \\
        0 & 2^{k-1} & 2^{k-1}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
