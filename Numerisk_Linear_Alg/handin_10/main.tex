\subsection*{(a) Verify that $A$ is symmetric and explain why $A$ is diagonalizable with only
real eigenvalues}
Verifying that $A$ is symmetric can be done by looking at it and saying 'Yep that's symmetric', since $row_i == column_i$\\\\
Or verify it using python.
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
A = np.array([[0.5, 0.25, 0.25], 
              [0.25, 0.3, 0.45],
              [0.25, 0.45, 0.3]])

if np.array_equal(A, A.T):
    print(True)
# Output for reference
True
\end{lstlisting}
Per the spectral theorem 23.10~\cite{NUMLA} any real symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times x}$ there exist an orthogonal
 matrix $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ such that $A = V \Lambda V^T$ where $\Lambda$ is
 a diagonal matrix, and because we can factor the matrix~\eqref{A} into that, then it can be said to be diagonalizable.\\
 To go even further, the Theorem also states that $A$ has $n$ real eigenvalues and an orthonormal basis consisting of it's egenvectores...Yeah take that 7-year old Gauss! I'm smart too!

\subsection*{(b) Verify that 1.0 is an eigenvalue of $A$, and then determine the other eigen-values of $A$.}
The characteristic polynomial for $A$ is
\begin{equation*}
    p(\lambda) = \det(A-\lambda I_3) = 
    \det\begin{bmatrix}
        0.5 - \lambda & 0.25 & 0.25 \\
        0.25 & 0.3 - \lambda & 0.45 \\
        0.25 & 0.45 & 0.3 - \lambda 
    \end{bmatrix}\\
\end{equation*}

\begin{align*}
    &= (0.5- \lambda) \det\begin{bmatrix}
        0.3 - \lambda & 0.45 \\
        0.45 & 0.3 -\lambda
    \end{bmatrix} - (0.25) \det\begin{bmatrix}
        0 & 0.15 - \lambda \\
        0.25 & 0.3 - \lambda
    \end{bmatrix} + (0.25)\det\begin{bmatrix}
        0.25 & 0.3 - \lambda \\
        0.25 & 0.45
    \end{bmatrix}\\ % skip some middle calculations
    &= ((0.3-\lambda) \cdot (0.3- \lambda))- (0.45 \cdot 0.45))
\end{align*}
